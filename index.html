A Queens Infrastructure Walking Tour

How Technology and Infrastructure Transformed Queens

A Walking Tour · Tahmid Islam · U.S. History

Queens' diversity was built. Not born.

"Queens' history is the history of trains, planes and cars."

— Jeffrey Kroessler, Rural County, Urban Borough

Infrastructure does not follow people - it directs them. The subway system, the highways, the World's Fair, and the urban planning decisions of Robert Moses collectively created the physical conditions that made Queens the most ethnically diverse place on Earth. The diversity visible today is the endpoint of over a century of deliberate choices.

Why This Tour

I was born in Bangladesh and came to Queens at age four. I grew up in Jackson Heights eating at the same Bengali restaurants, walking past the same vendors on Roosevelt Avenue, and seeing the same shops outside Diversity Plaza - and I never once asked why any of it was there.

Diversity was simply life, so it seemed like it had always been that way for everyone. This project revealed just how wrong that assumption was. What shocked me most was not any single fact, but how many choices had been made: choices to build, choices of policy, choices of planning, by people who never thought about me, my parents, or anyone like us.

Robert Moses did not plan for a Bangladeshi immigrant community. He planned for American progress without them. And yet here we are, on the 7 train, in Diversity Plaza, in a borough whose creation he helped make possible.

That contradiction - that systems built to exclude can become the foundation others build lives upon - is the most honest thing I learned this year about how American history actually works.
